The Sine Rule

In any triangle, the ratio of a side to the sine of its opposite angle is the same for all three pairs. Here's a proof in four steps — drag the vertices to watch the three ratios stay locked together.

// the proof

Four steps

iSetup
A triangle: three angles, three sides.
Label the vertices A, B, C. Each side is named for the angle opposite it: side a faces A, b faces B, c faces C.
iiConstruction
Drop a perpendicular from C to side AB.
Call its length $h$. This one line splits the triangle into two right-angled triangles — and that's the whole trick.
iiiTwo views of h
Measure the same height two ways.
In the left right-triangle (shaded blue), $\sin A = h/b$, so:
$$h = b\,\sin A$$
In the right one (shaded green), $\sin B = h/a$, so:
$$h = a\,\sin B$$
ivEquate
The two heights must agree.
Set them equal and divide by $\sin A\,\sin B$:
$$b\,\sin A = a\,\sin B \;\Longrightarrow\; \frac{a}{\sin A} = \frac{b}{\sin B}$$
Dropping the perpendicular from a different vertex gives $c/\sin C$ too. Hence:
$$\frac{a}{\sin A} = \frac{b}{\sin B} = \frac{c}{\sin C}$$
